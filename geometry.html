Geometry for
the blind

Project overview

The project required us to conduct a study on blind or disabled people to know about their everyday tasks and challenges. This process has been completed through research, interview and observational study.
A Geometry box was then been selected and modified to make it more easy and beneficial for the user.

User centric Design Service Design Product Design

Duration: 1 month Team type: Solo

User research and Documentation

User study was conducted in the Vinyasa Trust for Differently Challenged to understand the difficulties they face. We were able to communicate to four college students who shared their daily routines and hobbies. They gave insightful answers when asked about how they perform certain tasks.

Major insights derived about their hobbies, education, navigation, fashion and other aspects of their lives. Another user from Ramaiah Institute shared more on education, which is what drove me to pick the Geometry box.

“Throughout my school life very little products were made for us and hence we had to use regular stationery to do tasks. Special scales and geometry boxes were not provided. Lab experiments were difficult. Braillers are used by very few people because of it’s bulkiness.
Stylus and traditional paper is preferred.”

Pain points

The regular Geometry box may be great for you, but here is how it is nearly unusable for the blind.

  • Accessibility Features: Many geometry tools lack accessibility features such as tactile markings or auditory cues, making them inaccessible to blind individuals. There are no additional instruments provided to punch a line as the visually impaired can not see the line they drew.
  • Safety concerns: The corners of a scale are sometimes sharp. Similarly, the compass and divider ends are very sharp and have only one protective tip provided. The compass serves no purpose at all for blind people.
  • Accuracy: Due to the lack of tactile markings, there is no assurance of correctness while using the instruments.
  • Form: The instruments are stacked one over the other. This causes frustration and confusion while reaching out for the instruments.

Iterations

The tactile and functional geometry kit by Anupam Kumar Garg has been designed in such a way that it creates indents on the paper and that the student can make meaningful diagrams.
Considering this as my base model, a few iterations were made to make it more accessible.
(To begin with, this prototype is made from the measurement range of3 to 6.5cm.)

  • Point marker is made much simpler and easy to use.
  • Instead of using a frame holder and one circle frame to draw one circle, the two products are combined into one and extra circle is added as well.
  • Similarly, the arc makers instead of being singular have been made into a 4 in 1 product.
  • The slate to perform the geometrical constructions has been replaced with a magnetic slate+ binder clips to change orientation as the original only supports portrait.
  • This can also be used under any book, instead of just a sheet of paper.

Solution

With molding and magnetic materials, the final product was created.
